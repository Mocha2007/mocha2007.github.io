I'm not going to argue from a platform or political perspective, but a statistical one. All of the polling data showed that the election was close - nearly as close as the 2000 election. There were numerous known routes where Trump could win - eg. "secret trump voters", the so-called "blue wall", speaking of which...

The blue wall was always unstable.

Based on polling data from before the election, each blue wall state was the equivalent of rolling one six-sided die... and hoping to not roll a one or two. Now, granted, most of the dice won't be ones... but it'd be more surprising if one or two didn't flip. Keep in mind, the blue wall consisted of up to seven states (depending on who you ask), so we would expect two or three to flip on average (in the actual election, five did). Statistically speaking, the results of the election were higher than the average, but not obscenely so.

Actual Arguments

You can take these following seriously. Here I use evidence and citations to move points. Be warned though, I might use the elusive sarcasm, so make sure not to be a Sheldon McBuzzkill.

No, "Animals have feelings too" does not make Omnivorism Immoral

Probably the most convincing and least emotion-based philosophical argument for veganism I have found is some variation of the following:

Eating meat is wrong because there is no reason to place animals under humans.
They (well, many of them) have feelings and emotions too.

So let's break this down. Basically, there are two parts to this argument: that there is no reason to place animals under humans, and that animals have feelings/emotions/some other quality that is reason to avoid consuming them. Now, I take no issue with the latter half - many species of animals have been demonstrated to possess the capacity for the critical thinking, feelings, and emotions that defines intelligence and sentience. Thus we come back to the first half - that there is no reason to place animals under humans. And this argument, that I will now present, is one I have never seen used, either because the research is comparatively new, nobody's thought of it, or a combination of both.

Now, let's think for a moment. What do vegans eat for sustenance? Well, mostly plants, often with some fungi and supplements. I'm going to completely ignore the whole "veganism is expensive" argument since it's been done to death, so you had better thank me for that. If you agree that animals having aforementioned characteristics is reason enough to avoid eating animals, then if I were to demonstrate plants were also capable of these abilities, you would have to agree that plants should be avoided too. And as you may expect, this is exactly what I am about to demonstrate:

  • Plants feel pain - Howstuffworks
  • Plants respond to pain - Collective Evolution
  • Plant communication - NIH
  • Plants are capable of communication - Wired
  • Plants use chemical, visual, and possibly acoustic communication - National Geographic
  • Plants have memory - National Geographic
  • Plants have long-term memory and can learn - Sci-News
  • Plants have senses and memory - Scientific American
  • Plants remember and respond to stimuli - Popular Science

Well then. It seems we are at an impasse. In fact, there is evidence that plants may even communicate via exchange of genetic material, something animals are incapable of. The only thing not mentioned here already is emotion, something notoriously difficult to define. Nevertheless, Wikipedia defines emotion as so:

Emotion is any conscious experience characterized by intense mental activity and a high degree of pleasure or displeasure.

Several of the aforementioned articles (particularly the ones on pain) mention that plants respond to pain (displeasure). The other part of this definition is "intense mental activity". Like even quite a few animals, plants do not have a "brain" in the traditional sense. It seems our best understanding is that "thought" takes place throughout the plant. Of course, this is just attempting to project antropocentric concepts onto nonhuman beings - which brings us full circle. There is no objective way to say that how plants experience reality is inferior to animals, especially since the evidence suggests the opposite: that, in many ways, plants can sense, think, and communicate in more ways than any animal can - including humans.

So next time you try to suggest animals are inherently superior to plants, just know that your ferns are probably emitting the chemical equivalent of the bird.
