Pata

A fun Art/Auxlang.

Motivation

Toki Pona is a very good example of what a "simple" auxlang should look like. However, I feel like several core structural features could've been better, and, additionally, a larger vocabulary was totally necessary. After all, while Toki Pona may only have 125-ish words, it has far more lemmata, since you have to remember all of the word combinations for more complex items. I attempt to eliminate that need here. My biggest gripe with Toki Pona, overall, is that it's easy to learn, but hard to use.

I also used small elements of Lojban as an inspiration, particularly with the grammar philosophy.

In terms of differences from most auxlangs, I've decided to use VSO word order. This way, the head is always on the leftmost part of a phrase. Additionally, I call the different word classes "nouns", and "(in)transitive verbs", but logically, they more act all like verbs, with valencies of zero, one, and two, respectively. In the dictionary, only the noun forms are listed; the verb forms are simply derived from the nouns via replacing the final vowel. Furthermore, verbs can take verb phrases as arguments! So you can have a sentence which is structured vaguely like so: 

"The man who is walking has a dog named Brian."
has(walk(man) as(name(Ø dog) Brian))

And each of the constituents can act as their own sentences themselves: 

walk(man) → The man is walking.
as(name(Ø dog) Brian) → The dog is named Brian.
name(Ø dog) → The dog is named.

This way, even if you know none of the words in the sentence, you will always be able to draw a syntax tree. Always. No exceptions.

Phonology

There are 10 consonants, and 5 vowels. Vowel qualities match that of the IPA. The voiced plosives should, ideally, be pronounced nasally in the coda, and plosively in the onset. However, since this is an auxlang... I mean, whatever works for you, man. The syllable structure is, of course... (C)V(N)!

Morphology

  • There are "two" types of words: nouns (-a), and verbs (-i, -u, -e, -o; for 1-4 argument verbs respectively).
  • The adpositions of other languages correspond, typically, to transitive verbs here, and adjectives as intransitive.

Special Stems

These stems do not act grammatically different from others, but serve very important roles and are kept here fore safekeeping. Often times, these do not have a "simple" gloss.

d-

Main List

ad-
Etymology: en. (and numerous other European languages) in, fr. dans, jp. ni and naka
  1. in, inside
  2. x1 (is) in x2
as-
Etymology: en. as
  1. like, likeness
  2. x1 is like x2
dad-
Etymology: en. nose, nostril, schnoz and snot
  1. nose, smell, nasal
  2. x1 smells x2
gib-
Etymology: en. gib and give
  1. gift, give
  2. x1 gives x2 to x3
kas-
Etymology: en. cash
  1. money, cash
  2. x1 is monetary
pat-
Etymology: nl. praat, fr. patois
  1. tongue, speech, language, say, tell, speak
  2. x1 speaks with x2 about x3 in x4
tit-
Etymology: From the position of the tongue, influenced by en. teeth
  1. teeth, dental, bite
